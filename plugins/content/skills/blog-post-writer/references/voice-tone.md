# Peter Souter's Voice and Tone

Captured from analyzing posts at petersouter.xyz

## Core Writing Characteristics

### Voice

Conversational, self-aware, and technically grounded with a strong emphasis on humility. Peter writes as a pragmatic practitioner — "Just some guy y'know?" — rather than positioning himself as an authority. He prioritizes reader accessibility and intellectual honesty over signaling expertise.

### Vulnerability

Peter openly admits uncertainty, incomplete knowledge, and confusion. He never pretends to have all the answers.

Examples:

- "Honestly, I'm not a golang developer, I just like it for CLI apps..."
- "I've not really been in the nitty gritty"
- "I only found reference in this Github issue"
- "I just haven't got around to automating more yet"
- "maybe I'm out-of-step" (when disagreeing with a speaker)
- "This is an ambitious list I'll admit, and I'm sure it'll evolve throughout the year."

### Narrative Structure

Posts often follow a practitioner's journey: personal problem or context → exploration/attempt → honest assessment → forward-looking conclusion. Trip reports use thematic clustering within chronological structure. Technical posts use progressive complexity — concept first, then implementation.

### Sentence Style

- Mix of short punchy sentences and longer explanatory ones
- Uses fragments and interjections for emphasis: "All good right? Wrong!"
- Parenthetical asides for conversational texture: "(way back in 2011)", "(especially compared to my previous experiences)"
- Em-dashes for inline clarifications and asides
- Varies paragraph length for rhythm
- Liberal use of bullet points and lists for accessibility

### Specific Phrases and Patterns

**Opening hooks:**

- Leads with personal context or a practical problem, not dramatic hooks
- "One thing you should know about me is I love tinkering"
- "One of my hobbies is writing little CLI apps"
- "One of the more fiddly parts I found when using a custom provider..."
- "I've had to setup 3 different macbooks from scratch recently."
- Establishes personal stakes before diving into technical content

**Transitions:**

- "So with that in mind..."
- "For my next trick..."
- "Enter Boxen," "Enter Strap," "Enter: sck" (introducing tools as characters)
- "Notably," "Serendipitously," "Unfortunately"
- Uses section headers as natural pauses rather than forced transitions

**Emphasis techniques:**

- Single-line paragraphs for impact: "Boom, one deadline down"
- ALL CAPS sparingly for humorous emphasis: "DO THE DAMN THING"
- Emojis used sparingly but naturally (😆, not littered throughout)
- Italics for internal thoughts or emphasis
- Bold for key concepts

**Humor style:**

- Self-deprecating observations, not forced jokes
- Blunt reactions to errors: "Uh… what?"
- Noting absurdity in passing: "I've never heard a conference speaker swear so much 😆"
- Playful irreverence about his own plans and habits
- Humorous accountability stakes: threatening to post "GenAI Hustleslop" if he doesn't follow through

### Technical Writing Style

**Code and tools:**

- Names tools and projects explicitly with links for reader exploration
- Shows actual commands, code snippets, and error output
- Provides "before/after" comparisons to demonstrate improvement
- Code blocks precede explanations — shows first, explains second
- Contextualizes tools: explains *why* something matters, not just *what* it does

**Examples:**

- Inline code formatting: `terraform`, `brew cask`, `rg`, `fzf`
- Commands shown naturally in prose
- Tool names used casually, introduced like characters: "Enter Boxen"
- Error messages quoted directly to show real debugging experience

**Problem-first framing:**

- Identifies pain points before presenting solutions
- "acceptance tests don't really grok as much for me"
- "If Boxen went wrong, it was me people came to fix it."
- Shows the frustration, then the resolution

### Honesty About Limitations

Peter admits when things aren't perfect and qualifies his recommendations:

- "Overall, Aruba might be a bit of an overkill tool...but I've found it super useful for my usecases."
- "Maybe as I get more comfortable with Golang I'll switch..."
- "I know, I know, blogging about GenAI, how original"
- Openly acknowledges when he missed conference sessions: "I missed the ignites because I wanted to grab lunch"
- Admits incomplete knowledge without apologizing for it

### Self-Awareness and Humor

Light self-deprecation woven into technical content:

- "Just some guy y'know?" as a recurring self-identification
- Acknowledges the irony of his own topics: "blogging about GenAI, how original, and it's funny!"
- References "comfie" dotfiles from years ago with nostalgic humor
- Mini-rants about tools (GPG setup) balanced with acknowledging valid counterarguments
- Creates comedic stakes for accountability rather than being preachy

### Handling Disagreement

When Peter disagrees, he does it with intellectual humility:

- Acknowledges the opposing view first
- States his position without dismissing the other person
- Uses hedging language: "maybe I'm out-of-step"
- "I disagree massively on the ideas around chunks of work" — direct but not hostile
- Avoids performative critique

### Structure Patterns

**Sections often use:**

- Clear descriptive headers that guide the reader
- Numbered lists for sequential processes
- Bullet points for parallel information
- Code examples with surrounding context
- Photos and screenshots in trip reports (visual storytelling)
- Links to resources, CFP pages, slides, and videos for deeper engagement

**Common section patterns:**

- Personal context/problem → Tool exploration → Honest assessment → Forward look
- Trip reports: Day-by-day with thematic groupings and "General Takeaways" synthesis
- Technical posts: Problem statement → Progressive implementation → Validation/results
- Memoir-like historical walkthroughs for tool evolution posts

### Conclusions

Endings are understated and forward-looking rather than grand summaries:

- "Hopefully you enjoyed this walk down memory lane...hopefully you've seen the advantages."
- "I loved being back, and will be planning on attending (and likely submitting again!) for 2027"
- "This is an ambitious list I'll admit, and I'm sure it'll evolve throughout the year."
- Leaves room for reader disagreement and interpretation
- Invites continued dialogue rather than declaring final verdicts
- Positions readers as beneficiaries of lessons learned, not passive recipients

### Authenticity Markers

**Real examples:**

- Specific projects, GitHub issues, and PRs with links
- Actual error messages and debugging output
- Conference names, speaker names, talk titles
- Concrete details: tool versions, specific commands run, timelines
- Photos from events — campus aesthetics, not just slides

**Transparency about AI usage:**

- Dedicates sections to acknowledging AI tool usage (Claude Code, Gemini, CodeRabbit)
- Values-driven approach to crediting tools used in writing process

**Acknowledges help:**

- Credits collaborators and references community resources
- Links generously to others' work and talks

### Tone Shifts

Peter modulates tone based on content:

- **Technical tutorials**: Clear, instructional, problem-first, still conversational
- **Personal reflections**: Vulnerable, self-deprecating, honest about struggles
- **Trip reports**: Enthusiastic but critically engaged, photo-heavy, thematically organized
- **Tool reviews**: Enthusiastic but honest about limitations, experience-grounded
- **Planning posts**: Optimistic with self-aware hedging about ambition

### What to Avoid

- Corporate speak or marketing language
- Overly formal academic tone
- Pretending to have all the answers
- Dismissing reader concerns
- Being preachy or condescending
- Hiding uncertainty or mistakes
- False expertise — never claim deep knowledge where you don't have it
- Jargon dumps without context
- Evangelistic recommendations — ground everything in personal experience

### Key Principles

1. Be conversational but substantive — "Just some guy y'know?"
2. Share the journey, not just the destination
3. Admit when you're uncertain or learning — this builds trust
4. Use specific details: real tools, real commands, real error messages
5. Balance technical depth with accessibility
6. End with forward momentum, not grand conclusions
7. Write for a peer, not a student
8. Vulnerability builds credibility — own your gaps
9. Humor works when it's self-aware and observational
10. Practical value matters — every post should help the reader do something
11. Show your work: link to resources, credit tools, share the debugging process
12. Qualify recommendations honestly — what works for you may not work for others
